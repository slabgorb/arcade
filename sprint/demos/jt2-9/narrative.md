# Narrative

## Problem Statement
**Problem:** The first live playtest of the two-player Joust demo turned up four visible glitches at once: characters' feet sank into the platforms they were standing on, characters never turned to face the direction they were moving, joust duels felt like they rewarded facing the wrong way, and Player 2 rendered detached from its mount instead of riding it. **Why it matters:** these are the first things a player notices in the opening seconds of a demo — a knight standing ankle-deep in a ledge or frozen facing one direction breaks the illusion immediately, and it undermines confidence in all the physics and collision work already shipped in the prior stories.

## What Changed
- **Ledge seat:** Sprites were being drawn from a "feet" reference point with no adjustment for each character's actual art — like taping a photo to a wall using the corner of the frame instead of the corner of the picture. We pulled the exact per-sprite vertical offset from the original 1982 game's data (different for a standing ostrich, a flying buzzard, a running stork, etc.) and applied it at render time, so every character now sits flush on the platform it's standing on.
- **Turning/facing:** Pushing left or right updated the character's internal "which way am I facing" state, but nothing downstream ever read it — the drawing code always drew the character facing right, no matter which way they were walking. We connected that missing wire: in the air, facing now flips instantly with the joystick; on the ground, it flips through a "skid and turn" animation that was already built and fully tested from an earlier story, but had never actually been reachable from a controller — like a light switch that was wired to the fixture but never connected to the wall plate.
- **Joust-facing feel:** Players reported that colliding backwards still won duels. Before changing anything, we re-checked the original 1982 game's compiled logic line-by-line and confirmed it: the original game decides joust winners by height alone — facing direction was never part of the rule, even in 1982. So we made no change to that rule. The "wrong way wins" feeling was actually a symptom of the turning bug above — once a player can actually face the direction they're moving, joust outcomes line up with expectations without touching the win/lose math at all.
- **Render polish:** Fixed a second render bug caught during this same testing pass — Player 2's stork mount was floating about 17 pixels above its rider (nearly a rider's-height gap); it's now seated with only a 1-pixel gap, matching Player 1's ostrich. We also documented (rather than invented) that the "front row vs. back row" depth rule for platforms is a reasonable simplification, not a rule from the original game — the original never encoded per-platform depth data, so pretending otherwise would be fabricating fidelity that doesn't exist.
- **Leftovers:** Capped an internal gameplay-event log so it can't grow without bound during a long play session, and logged a harmless leftover naming mismatch in wing-collision data for future cleanup.

## Why This Approach
Every number in this fix — how far to lift a sprite, whether facing affects a joust win — was pulled from the original arcade game's source data rather than adjusted by eye. "Looks about right" doesn't hold up over time and can't be re-verified later; a cited number can. That discipline is why, when the joust-feel complaint came in, engineering re-checked the ROM before touching the win/lose rule — it would have been easy to "fix the feel" by adding a facing penalty, but that would have been a wrong fix that happened to mask the real bug (turning). Verifying first avoided shipping an inauthentic rule change to solve a problem that lived somewhere else. Similarly, rather than inventing a precise per-platform depth number the original game never used, the current approximation is documented honestly as a stand-in — accurate about what it is, not dressed up as more faithful than it can be.

## Before/After
| Area | Before | After |
|---|---|---|
| Ledge seat | Feet sink into the platform by an amount that varies per sprite (unmeasured, inconsistent) | Every sprite sits flush on its platform, offset derived from the original game's own data |
| Turning | Character always renders facing right, regardless of movement direction | Character visibly turns to face left or right, both in the air and on the ground |
| Joust feel | Backwards collisions appeared to win unfairly, with no clear cause | Confirmed the win/lose rule is correct and unchanged (verified against the original game); the "backwards wins" feeling resolves once turning works |
| Two-player render | Player 2 renders as a small, indistinctly colored sprite disconnected from the platform and its mount (~17px float) | Player 1 and Player 2 render in clearly distinct colors, each correctly seated on its own mount (~1px gap) |
| Stability | 1018 automated checks passing | 1038 automated checks passing, zero regressions in existing behavior |
